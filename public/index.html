Understanding Advance Care Planning

What Advance Care Planning Actually Is

Advance care planning is not primarily about paperwork — it is a process of reflection and communication. The Institute for Healthcare Improvement defines it as "a process that supports adults at any age or stage of health in understanding and sharing their personal values, life goals, and preferences regarding future medical care." The document that results is important, but the conversation that produces it is what makes it meaningful.

Only about 37% of US adults have completed any advance directive (CDC, 2022). Among those who have not, the most common reason is "I haven't gotten around to it" — not opposition to the idea. Sage removes the friction by making the conversation feel like a natural extension of life review, not a legal exercise. The resulting document is a byproduct of the conversation, not the goal of it.

Living Will, POLST, and Healthcare Proxy: What Each Does

Three documents work together to protect your wishes: A living will states which treatments you do or do not want if you cannot speak for yourself. It is instructional but may not be immediately actionable in an emergency. A healthcare proxy (or durable power of attorney for healthcare) designates a person who can make real-time decisions when you cannot — filling the gaps your living will cannot anticipate. A POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) translates your wishes into immediate medical orders signed by a physician, carried with you across all care settings.

For most people, all three are worth completing. The living will and proxy protect future decisions; the POLST protects present emergencies. A physician from co-op.care can help determine which documents are most appropriate for your current situation.

The Research on What Makes a Death Good

A 2015 systematic review in the British Medical Journal identified consistent themes across 36 qualitative studies of what constitutes a good death: being in a preferred location, freedom from pain and distress, clarity of communication, emotional and spiritual support, presence of loved ones, and a sense that life had meaning. These are not outcomes medicine delivers automatically — they require planning and explicit communication.

Families of patients who completed advance care planning reported significantly less decisional conflict, less anxiety, and lower rates of complicated grief compared to families of patients who did not (JAGS, 2010). The benefit is not just for the patient — it is a gift given to the people left behind. CareGoals captures the life story alongside the medical wishes, preserving both for the family.

When to Start — and Why Earlier Is Better

Advance care planning is not only for people who are seriously ill. Accidents, sudden illness, and acute events can affect anyone at any age. The American Bar Association recommends completing a healthcare proxy designation at age 18 — the point at which parents lose automatic legal authority to make medical decisions for their children.

For older adults, the conversation becomes more urgent after a hospitalization, a new serious diagnosis, or a significant functional decline. Medicare covers Advance Care Planning (ACP) as a separately billable service (CPT 99497, 99498) — meaning your physician can spend dedicated time facilitating this conversation with you, and Medicare will pay for it. co-op.care's physician partner facilitates ACP conversations as part of the membership.

Storing and Sharing Your Directive

A completed advance directive is only useful if it can be found and accessed when needed. Common failure points: documents stored in a safe deposit box that cannot be accessed on a weekend, photocopies that lack legal signatures, and documents that exist but are unknown to the treating medical team. The National POLST Paradigm recommends that POLST forms be immediately accessible — typically in a visible location in the home or carried by the patient.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a living will and an advance directive? add
An advance directive is the broad category; a living will is one type. A living will specifies which treatments you do or do not want. A healthcare proxy designates a person to decide for you. A POLST translates your wishes into immediate physician-signed medical orders. Most people benefit from having all three. Sage helps you create all of them through conversation.
Can I change my advance directive later? add
Yes, at any time while you have decision-making capacity. To revoke, notify your healthcare providers, destroy existing copies, and document the revocation in writing. Provide updated copies to your physician, hospital, and designated proxy. CareGoals makes updating as simple as continuing the conversation.
Is a POLST different from a DNR? add
Yes. A DNR order covers only CPR. A POLST is a comprehensive medical order covering CPR, mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition, hospitalization preferences, and the level of medical intervention desired — signed by a physician and valid across all care settings. POLST is most appropriate for seriously ill individuals or those in the last year of life.
Does my advance directive work in all states? add
Laws vary by state. Most states have provisions to honor out-of-state documents if they meet general validity requirements, but this is not guaranteed. If you divide time between states, executing documents compliant with both states' requirements is advisable. co-op.care supports remote online notarization (RON) across 35–40 states.
Who should be my healthcare proxy? add
Choose someone who will honor your wishes even under emotional pressure — not necessarily the closest family member. They should be geographically accessible in a crisis, willing to advocate assertively with medical teams, able to tolerate uncertainty, and genuinely familiar with your values. Discuss your wishes explicitly before designating them.
What is a quality death? add
Research consistently identifies common elements: dying in a preferred location, freedom from unnecessary pain, presence of loved ones, a sense that life had meaning, and having wishes honored. Clarity is a gift you give your family. Quality Death is built on the principle that talking about the end of life is an act of love.

Is the document legally binding? add
A document becomes legally binding when it meets execution requirements of the applicable state — typically two witnesses (who are not healthcare providers or beneficiaries) or notarization.
